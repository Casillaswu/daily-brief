01 · Lead Capital / Product ○Neutral US · PM impact

Prediction markets' impact on traditional gambling showed on two fronts this week: investors shorting betting stocks are up $2.3B this year, and PM leader Kalshi expanded into crypto finance

Bettors Insider · CNBC · Casino.org · 2026-05-26 ~ 05-29

Prediction markets (PMs — platforms that let you bet on whether something will happen, like Kalshi and Polymarket) are seen as traditional sports betting's main competitive threat. That threat advanced on two fronts this week. First, capital markets: hedge funds heavily shorting (betting the price will fall) online gambling stocks are up $2.3B (about TWD 73.6bn) YTD. Shorting Flutter (FanDuel's parent, UK-listed) earned ~$2B; DraftKings (DKNG) ~$351M (about TWD 1.1bn); Entain ~$35M. Flutter is down nearly 56% YTD, DKNG and Entain each ~30%. The reason is blunt: PMs siphoning traditional sportsbook customers, plus the UK raising online betting tax to 40% in April crushing margins — the market is betting with its money that "PMs win, traditional sportsbook loses." Second, product positioning: Kalshi (valued $22B, about TWD 704bn) announced on 5/29 it's expanding its product line into "perpetual futures" (a no-expiry financial future) and crypto trading. Kalshi keeps getting sued by US states for being illegal gambling, so this pushes its "financial exchange" positioning further, to keep state gambling laws away. Note: this is a product-line expansion, not an exit from sports betting — Kalshi's sports markets keep running.


Bottom line: mixed for pure online sportsbook brands, +EV mid-term for licensed sportsbooks. Together: the market sees PMs' threat to traditional sportsbook as real (hence the shorts), while the PM leader is strengthening its financial positioning to fully separate from "gambling." This cuts two ways for licensed gambling: short-term, if PMs successfully use a "financial" identity to bypass gambling regulation, that's a long-term competitive threat; mid-term, the more Kalshi moves to crypto finance, the higher its product bar and the further it drifts from ordinary sports bettors, diluting the direct threat to traditional sportsbook retail. Action: (1) PH/Brazil compliance messaging can add "even Kalshi is leaning on a crypto expansion to reinforce its financial positioning, which itself shows 'PMs aren't gambling' is contestable"; (2) while betting stocks are beaten down, watch which listed firms cut emerging-market budgets — talent and M&A loosen; (3) the stock drop is mainly about "PMs threat + UK high tax," two specific issues unrelated to emerging-market online fundamentals — Brazil traffic +237% this year is the counterexample.

Signal 02 · Brazil · Regulation −Negative

Brazil Senate passes PL 2.985 on 5/28: bans athlete and influencer endorsements, live odds only in the operator's own app

Summary: Brazil's Senate fast-tracked PL 2.985/2023 on 5/28. Three key rules: (1) no celebrity endorsements — no athletes, influencers or artists in betting ads, except athletes retired 5+ years; (2) live odds only inside the operator's own site/app, to curb impulsive betting; (3) push and SMS need written consent first. The bill now moves to the lower house and may change, but the Senate clearing it fast means "ads getting tightened further" is all but locked in.


Bottom line: -EV for operators relying on "celebrity endorsements + live odds everywhere" (Betano, Pixbet, bet365 and similar heavy-endorsement brands all hit), +EV for those built on product. This is the 5/28 vote tracked in W21 — it passed. Lula's regulation moved from "managing players" to "managing ads." Action: endorsement-driven operators should review the 30-90 day exit clauses in endorsement contracts soon; pause sponsorship signings until the lower-house version is set; route live-odds creative back to the operator's own app; a "product-not-endorsements" template is worth preparing, with Betano as a precedent. iGamiST · Read original →

Signal 03 · Philippines · Regulation −Negative

PAGCOR supplier accreditation deadline 5/31: non-filers can't go live from 6/1, non-licensed systems force-shut from 8/1

Summary: PAGCOR's (Philippine gaming regulator) board ruled on 5/21: all upstream suppliers (firms doing systems, platforms, game content, payments, KYC for operators) must file license applications by 5/31. Those who filed by 5/31 may keep supplying while under review (to 7/31); non-filers can no longer go live and supply from 6/1. Final deadline 7/31 — from 8/1, suppliers without a license get their systems force-shut (the original word is decommission — literally pulling the plug, not a fine).


Bottom line: -EV for small operators leaning on unlicensed offshore suppliers, +EV for suppliers already on PAGCOR's list, and a higher compliance bar for the PH market overall. This is the first gate of the "7/31 deadline" tracked in W21 — it closed today. Action: operators should confirm today whether each upstream supplier filed by 5/31; non-filers are cut off from 6/1 — line up replacements; "our whole tech stack is on PAGCOR's list" can be a trust point for affiliates and high-value players. Signal 04 · Cross-market · Responsible Gaming ＋Positive

Two "protect the player" stories point the same way: PH opens a gambling-addiction helpline, CNN reports youth hooked on PMs

Summary: these are two separate stories from two countries, but they point at one thing — regulators and public opinion are both tightening "protect the player, prevent addiction" this week. (1) PH's PAGCOR opened a national 24-hour gambling-addiction helpline on 5/27 (line 02-8248-9568), confidential counseling for addicts and families; (2) US CNN reported on 5/28 that 18-21-year-olds are pouring into Kalshi and Polymarket — because these platforms are regulated as "financial exchanges," not gambling, so they dodge state gambling age limits (most US states require 21). It cited a 21-year-old who lost "five figures."


Bottom line: +EV for licensed operators, -EV for PMs, -EV for industry ad and age-verification rules (going stricter). Why it's positive for the licensed industry, the causal chain: CNN's piece points squarely at the loophole of "PMs using a financial identity to dodge gambling age limits" → public opinion pushes regulators to close that loophole → the tightening lands on the loophole-exploiting PMs, not on compliant operators. Licensed operators already do full 21+ verification and already meet responsible-gaming requirements — this round of tightening hits the non-compliant competitors, raising the industry bar so compliant players relatively benefit. It's not that "addiction is good," it's that "this round of regulation and opinion targets loophole-exploiters, not the compliant." Action: PH operators can make "plugged into PAGCOR's official helpline + full 21+ verification" a trust point; self-screen creative for anything read as encouraging youth betting.
