01 · Lead Regulation ○Neutral North America · Prediction markets legal fight

The prediction-market legal fight goes both ways: Kalshi, Polymarket and the CFTC sue Minnesota, and the same week New Mexico sues Kalshi

Gambling Insider · CFTC · @WALLACHLEGAL · 2026-06-02 ~ 06-05

For six months the storyline was states suing Kalshi and Polymarket for illegal gambling. This week it flipped — but not simply into "platforms sue states"; both directions escalated at once. On the platform side: Minnesota's governor signed SF 3432, making it the first state to outlaw prediction markets (PMs — platforms to bet on whether something will happen, like Kalshi and Polymarket) by statute, effective August 1. The CFTC (Commodity Futures Trading Commission) sued Minnesota first, arguing PMs are federally regulated event contracts the state can't police; Kalshi (a CFTC-licensed prediction market) filed June 2; Polymarket (an offshore platform settled in crypto) became the third plaintiff June 3. The cases were consolidated, with a July 1 federal preliminary-injunction hearing; the CFTC also sued Wisconsin. On the state side, no retreat: New Mexico became the first state to take Kalshi to court and retain outside counsel — most states had only sent warning letters, so outside counsel signals a long fight.


Bottom line: short term +EV for the platforms (federal cover), +EV for PH / BR compliance messaging. The point isn't who wins — it's that "are prediction markets finance or gambling?" got pushed to a federal-court showdown, and July 1 sets the tone for PM regulation all half. Short term they get a federally backed shield; but if a federal court ultimately rules "event contracts are finance, outside gambling law," that opens a door for anyone wanting to skip a licence — unfair to licensed operators who pay for one. Moves: add a line to PH / BR compliance messaging — the US has to litigate to the federal level to decide whether PMs count as gambling, so "it's not gambling" is far from settled; watch the July 1 hearing AND New Mexico's state-court line together, draw no conclusions on the PM threat first; affiliates and B2Bs partnered with PM platforms should write "regulation unsettled" into contract risk clauses.

Signal 02 · UK · M&A ○Neutral

Bally's Intralot makes a firm £243.1M offer for Evoke (parent of William Hill, 888) on June 5

Summary: the "make a firm offer or walk away by June 8" deadline W22 tracked resolved early on June 5 — 52p per share, valuing Evoke at £243.1M (about NT$10.3bn), with the board recommending shareholders accept. Holders can take 52p cash or 0.537 new Bally's Intralot shares on Euronext Athens. The combined group projects FY25 net revenue of €3.2B (about NT$112bn). Bottom line: -EV for UK legacy bookmakers, +EV for pure-play emerging-market brands and senior talent. Same "UK legacy collapse releases talent" thread from W18 to W22, now moving from "in talks" to "signed." Integration over 6 to 12 months means layoffs and reshuffles, so senior talent loosens up. Moves: now through end-2026 is the window to recruit senior William Hill / 888 product and compliance talent, especially multi-market regulatory experience; watch which markets get cut post-integration — those customers and affiliates will look for a new home. Signal 03 · Brazil · Regulation −Negative

Brazil's ad-ban bill PL 2.985 reaches the Chamber and opens hearings; CBF publicly backs the current Bets Law

Summary: W22 reported the Senate passing PL 2.985 (banning athletes and influencers from endorsing betting) and sending it to the Chamber. This week the Chamber's Economic Development Committee held hearings on the Bets Law's impact since it took effect in January 2025 — with wide divisions among lawmakers. The same week, CBF (Brazilian Football Confederation) VP Michelle Ramalho publicly backed the current Bets Law at a Lisbon forum on June 2, urging the government to "focus enforcement on illegal operators" and questioning why MPs want to repeal it.


Bottom line: -EV for Brazilian operators relying on celebrity endorsements, mixed for those built on product and club sponsorship, +EV for compliant licensed players. Brazil's risk isn't "will it get stricter" but "which line tightens first." The CBF stepped in not to defend betting but to protect club sponsorship revenue — an endorsement ban would hit sponsorships, and football wants to keep that. So the advertising line gets stricter while the CBF helps shield club sponsorship. Moves: review 30-to-90-day exit clauses in endorsement contracts; shift budget from influencer endorsements to club sponsorship plus product; watch the committee's schedule as the signal for whether PL 2.985 accelerates. Signal 04 · Philippines · Market −Negative

PAGCOR chairman Tengco warns 2026 nationwide GGR could fall up to 19%; online segment already -22.4% in 1Q26

Summary: PAGCOR (the Philippine gaming regulator) chairman Tengco warned on June 5 that 2026 nationwide gaming GGR (gross gaming revenue — what players lose) could fall up to 19%, to ₱320–350B (about NT$176–193bn), versus 2025's record ₱396.1B (about NT$218bn). The online segment is worse — already -22.4% in 1Q26. The cause isn't demand drying up but licensed platforms being required to de-link from GCash and Maya, the two biggest e-wallets: the deposit path is cut, friction rises, and players churn. (Caught by igaming trigger monitor v2 on 2026-06-05; source asgam, figures cross-checked.)


Bottom line: -EV for pure-play online brands (Reagan's home market), but +EV for compliant brands that can solve the payment friction. The online segment's -22.4% isn't players quitting — it's a side effect of tightening compliance: PAGCOR is pushing licensed platforms to de-link from GCash and Maya (AML and responsible-gambling reasons), and the price is harder online deposits and player churn. Stacked on §5's July 31 supplier deadline, the Philippine online market is squeezed from two sides at once — supply-chain cleanup and payment de-linking. But flipped around: whoever can give players a smooth, compliant deposit path after the de-link takes the churned share. Moves: immediately audit your PH deposit flow's reliance on GCash and Maya and prepare alternative payment (bank transfer, other compliant wallets, cash top-up points); make "still-smooth deposits after the de-link" an acquisition pitch; stress-test models for the online segment keeping falling, don't budget off 2025's record; don't let -19% scare you into cutting PH budget — this is short-term bleeding from payment friction, not demand vanishing, and whoever solves payment takes share.

Signal 05 · Sports Betting ＋Positive

World Cup kicks off June 11: Brazilian fans can legally bet on their national team for the first time

Summary: the World Cup opens June 11 in Mexico City, with 48 teams, 104 matches, across the US, Canada and Mexico. Brazil's stakes differ from the US: this is Brazil's first World Cup since its 2025 legalization, and the first time fans can bet on their own team on licensed platforms — Brazil is +850 to win. The first World Cup after legalization, with the home team a favorite, is the lowest-cost acquisition stretch Brazil will see — miss it and wait four years. But mind Signal 03 — Brazil is tightening ads, so World Cup creative can't rely on player faces and must lean on match atmosphere and product. Moves: the days before June 11 are the last window to ship creative, Brazilian assets avoiding star faces; build a "register for World Cup offers, then play casino" funnel with retention and cross-sell baked in; Philippine creative must avoid "guaranteed win" language. Regulation−NegativePH · deadline countdown

PAGCOR supplier-deadline progress: June 1 declaration gate passed, July 31 at T-54

Inside Asian Gaming · 2026-06-01

W22 covered PAGCOR's (the Philippine regulator) supplier-accreditation timeline; this week brought the second gate: from June 1, all existing distributorship agreements must be declared to PAGCOR, and undeclared ones are automatically void. The final deadline remains July 31 — from August 1, any supplier that hasn't completed accreditation (fees, documents, on-site inspection, system testing, performance deposit) has its systems decommissioned (literally unplugged). Today (June 7) is T-54 to July 31.


Bottom line: -EV for small and mid-size operators relying on unlicensed offshore suppliers, +EV for compliant suppliers already on the PAGCOR list. Compliance barriers closing one by one. Moves: operators should immediately confirm whether each upstream supplier's distributorship agreement was declared by June 1 — undeclared ones are already void and need replacing; in June and July, unlicensed suppliers' assets get repriced, opening a window to grab their people and customers.

Sports Betting

Sports betting's substantive moves this week are all covered elsewhere: acquisition in Signal 05, product mix in §3, UK shirt sponsorship and the ban in §4. In one line — the World Cup is the biggest acquisition opportunity in years, but at the same time Brazil banned athlete and influencer endorsements (Signal 03) and the UK is set to ban betting-brand shirts (§4), so the two channels that most cheaply buy exposure are closing in Reagan's home market and mature markets at once.

Bottom line: -EV for operators acquiring by paying for star endorsements and shirt ad space, +EV for those acquiring via signup offers and the product itself. Plainly: to win new players at a World Cup, the fastest route used to be hiring a star to film an ad and printing the brand on the shirt — big, fast exposure. Now Brazil bans star faces and the UK bans shirt logos, so that fastest route is shut in both markets. What's left: pull players in with signup offers, hold attention with match content, then cross-sell new players into casino and slots for long-term retention. Moves: stop spending World Cup budget on star endorsements and shirt ad space; spend it on signup offers, product and retention — in Brazil and the UK that's now the only route left.
