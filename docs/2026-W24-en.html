CFTC Releases First Federal Prediction-Market Rule Proposal: Most Sports Contracts Allowed, Manipulatable & War/Assassination Contracts Banned

Covers · Sportico · CDC Gaming · @WALLACHLEGAL · 2026-06-10

Prediction markets (PMs — platforms where you bet on whether an event will happen, like Kalshi and Polymarket) had until now been fought out in court — states suing the platforms, platforms and the CFTC counter-suing states. This week is different: on 6/10 the CFTC (US Commodity Futures Trading Commission) released its first federal rule proposal — 267 pages, out after White House approval, with a 45-day public comment period. Not another lawsuit — the first time a regulator has actually put "how prediction markets should be governed" in writing. The proposal grades by contract type: most sports event contracts (moneyline, point spread, player props) are allowed in principle; but two categories are banned — easily manipulatable contracts (player injuries, officiating decisions, specific in-game events), and contracts on "whether a bad thing will happen" like war outcomes, terrorist attacks, assassinations, and the violent overthrow of governments. The same week states kept pushing: the Nevada Gaming Control Board filed a contempt motion against Kalshi, alleging it spent only ~$190K (about NT$6M) on geofencing while Nevada users could still bet on the NBA Finals and World Cup (see §9 NA).


Bottom line: mid-term mixed for PM platforms, +EV for the compliance narrative in the Philippines and Brazil. The proposal cuts both ways for PM platforms — short term they basically got what they wanted most ("sports contracts legalized"), a big win; but "case-by-case review + banning manipulatable contracts" also hands regulators a lever to tighten anytime, and states haven't conceded — Nevada went straight to a contempt motion. The point isn't that Kalshi won — it's that the framework "prediction markets count as finance, governed federally" got drafted for the first time, with a formal version due in 45 days. For licensees paying for PAGCOR and SPA licenses and playing by the rules, this actually turns "even the US is writing a dedicated rulebook, using IP geofencing, and still fighting contempt motions" into ready-made compliance ammunition. Action: add a line to PH/BR compliance narratives — the US is writing a 267-page rulebook for prediction markets, so "it's not gambling so it needs no license" hasn't even held up on US home turf; have agents and B2B partners working with PM platforms write "federal rule proposal, not finalized" into contract risk clauses; watch the formal version when the 45-day comment window closes (~end of July) — that's the real settling point.

Summary: Brazil's Secretariat of Prizes and Betting (SPA, Secretaria de Prêmios e Apostas) on 6/3 issued Technical Note SEI 3620/2026 to all licensed operators, covering communication, advertising and marketing during the World Cup (6/11–7/19). Key point: the note adds no new obligations — it "restates existing rules + announces coordinated, heightened enforcement throughout the tournament." SPA cares most about two things — monitoring foreign operators that are legal in the host countries (US/Canada/Mexico) but unlicensed in Brazil and exposing Brazilian users during the World Cup; and tight control over digital influencers and betting affiliates.


Bottom line: -EV for Brazil playbooks built on influencers and affiliates, +EV for licensees running product and mainstream media buys. Signal 04 · Philippines · Market −Negative

Philippines Triple Compliance-Cost Hit: MGF Monthly Fee Live 6/1, Credit-Card Gambling Fully Banned, 24-Hour Problem-Gambling Helpline

Summary: W23 was PAGCOR's (Philippine gaming regulator) GGR (gross gaming revenue) decline warning. This week's Philippine news is three compliance costs that have already landed: (1) The Minimum Guaranteed Franchise fee (MGF — a fixed monthly floor per license) went live 6/1 — e-casino operators pay a ₱9M/month floor (about NT$4.8M), sportsbooks and others ₱3M/month minimum; rising again from 2027. (2) Credit-card gambling is fully banned, officially to stop "borrow-to-bet debt spirals wrecking families." (3) PAGCOR opened a 24-hour national problem-gambling helpline on 6/1.


Bottom line: -EV for pure-online brands (the Philippines is a home market), relatively +EV for well-capitalized compliant brands that can carry fixed costs. Stacked on W23's online segment -22.4% in 1Q26 (deposit friction from licensed platforms decoupling from the two big e-wallets GCash and Maya), the Philippine online cost structure has flipped — from "take a cut of what you earn" to "pay a ₱9M floor every month whether you earn it or not." For small and mid-sized pure-online operators that's a direct squeeze: revenue falling, fixed costs rising, acquisition channels narrowing. But whoever survives this cost shakeout takes the share of those who exit. Action: rebuild unit economics treating MGF as a fixed cost (spread the ₱9M floor across current players — does ARPU cover it?); with credit cards banned, immediately check that alternative payments (bank transfer, compliant wallets, cash top-up points) flow smoothly and turn "compliant yet smooth top-up" into an acquisition selling point; don't retreat wholesale — survivors capture the exiters' share.Yogonet · Read original →
