01 · LEAD Regulation ○Neutral North America · Prediction Markets

Prediction-market litigation breaks out on multiple fronts: two-state AGs question CFTC authority, Polymarket joins the Minnesota suit, CME sues CFTC, July 1 hearing

Crypto Briefing · Courthouse News · Cryptopolitan · 2026-06-15 ~ 06-21

This week the litigation around prediction markets (PMs — platforms that let you bet on whether something will happen, like Kalshi and Polymarket) all landed at once. The attorneys general (AGs) of California and Minnesota jointly questioned whether the CFTC (US Commodity Futures Trading Commission) even has authority over prediction markets — the CFTC's position is "PMs are finance, so federal jurisdiction," and these two states disagree, arguing it belongs to the states. Polymarket, through its US arm Polymarket US, joined Kalshi and Trump in suing Minnesota's May ban, with the cases consolidated and a preliminary-injunction hearing set for July 1. And CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange, the world's largest futures exchange) turned around and sued the CFTC, arguing that Kalshi-style perpetual contracts (perpetuals — contracts with no expiry) should be classified as swaps (a more heavily regulated derivative), not allowed through as futures — the industry's own giant saying regulation is too loose.


Bottom line: mixed for PM platforms; +EV for licensed operators who pay to play by the rules. Right now the feds, states, and exchanges are each pulling a different way and it's unsettled who decides — the July 1 hearing is the first node with a visible outcome. For licensees who paid for PAGCOR and SPA licenses and operate by the rules, the US mess is actually useful: the US can't even agree whether prediction markets are gambling, and CME says regulators classified them wrong, so "PMs are inherently legal, no license needed" doesn't hold up even on home turf. Action: agents and B2Bs working with PM platforms should write "US feds, states, and exchanges still in court, legality undecided" into contract risk clauses; in the Philippines and Brazil, use this to answer "is paying for a license a disadvantage?" — the US is fighting this many lawsuits over prediction markets, so a license is the cost of entry, not a handicap; next, watch the July 1 hearing and the late-July end of the CFTC comment period.

Regulation○NeutralUS · Exchange counter-suit

CME sues CFTC: swap or futures for perpetuals, an industry giant questioning that the rules are too loose

Yahoo Finance · via Reuters · 2026-06

Signal 01 noted CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange, the world's largest futures exchange) suing the CFTC; here are the details. The dispute is whether Kalshi-style perpetual contracts (perpetuals — no expiry, maintained by a funding rate) should be classified as swaps (a more heavily regulated derivative) or, as the CFTC allowed, as futures. CME argues that allowing them as futures exposes retail to continuous funding-rate bleed and they should be swaps to protect retail. The point: this isn't an anti-gambling group suing — it's the world's largest futures exchange, i.e., the industry itself saying the CFTC's classification is wrong.


Bottom line: -EV for PM platforms, +EV for the "PM regulation undecided" narrative. When even an industry giant questions the classification, "prediction markets are a compliant financial product" gets shakier. Action: "even CME sued the CFTC over the classification" is strong material for emerging markets arguing "PM legality is undecided, don't rush to partner" — more persuasive than citing anti-gambling groups.

Product○Neutral🆕 PM event contracts

PMs turn the World Cup into "event contract" products: Kalshi leans on group-stage and player props

FinanceFeeds · 2026-06

Kalshi's World Cup volume surge wasn't a single outright market — it was slicing the tournament into many fine-grained products: highly liquid group-stage contracts and hyper-local player props (betting a single player's performance). That repackages traditional sports betting (spreads, totals, player data) as "event contracts" under CFTC regulation rather than state gaming licenses. By the numbers: Kalshi's opening-week notional volume (total contract face value) hit $5.1B, of which the World Cup topic accounted for $344M cumulatively by 6/17; in the same week, spot volume across all PM platforms and all topics hit a record $8.7B (about NT$270B). Note: this is prediction-market "volume," a different measure from the sportsbook "handle" in Signal 02 (one is financial contract face value, the other is the amount players wager) — they can't be compared directly.


Bottom line: +EV for the PM field (product validated by the World Cup), mixed for traditional licensed sportsbooks (one more product form competing for volume). PMs used to be the niche "bet on elections and news" tool; this World Cup proves they can run fine-grained, high-volume sports products. Action: study how Kalshi slices events into fine-grained, high-liquidity products — the "fine-grained, high-frequency" approach is reference material for your own in-play design; don't treat PMs as an unrelated lane, they compete for the same sports-betting users.
