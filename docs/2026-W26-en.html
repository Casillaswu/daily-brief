01 · Lead Regulation ○Neutral North America · Prediction Markets

The federal CFTC counter-sues Kentucky to assert federal jurisdiction over prediction markets

Yogonet · Front Office Sports · 2026-06-22 ~ 06-28

First, the background: there's a tug-of-war in the US over who regulates prediction markets (PMs — platforms that let people bet on whether an event will happen, like Kalshi and Polymarket). The federal financial regulator, the CFTC (Commodity Futures Trading Commission), says "these are financial products, federal jurisdiction"; many states say "this is really gambling, it's ours to regulate — or ban."

This week's move (6/23): Kentucky's Attorney General (AG) first sued the platforms, calling them "illegal sportsbooks," and passed a 14.25% transaction tax. The CFTC then counter-sued Kentucky to block these moves and make its "PMs are federal" position stick. Until now the states going after the platforms were Democratic-led; Kentucky is Republican-led, and its AG is Republican. So now states from both parties are pushing back. Kentucky is the 9th state the CFTC has sued; ~20 states are now in this litigation. That 14.25% tax isn't an outright ban — it taxes the platforms into being unprofitable, to push them out on their own.


Bottom line: mixed for the platforms, +EV for licensed operators that pay to stay compliant. Here's why — in the Philippines and Brazil, people often ask "is getting licensed and compliant a disadvantage?" This US fight is the counter-example: even in the US, even "is a prediction market gambling, and who regulates it" is still being fought across ~20 states with no settled answer. So "prediction markets are inherently legal, no license needed" doesn't hold — getting licensed is the price of entry, not a disadvantage. Action: affiliates and B2B partners working with PM platforms should write "US regulation unsettled, ~20 states litigating" into contract risk clauses; watch two dates next — the 7/1 Minnesota preliminary-injunction hearing and the end-of-July close of the CFTC comment period.

Signal 04 · Brazil · Regulation −Negative

Brazil opens a probe into World Cup betting ads: broadcaster CazeTV is the first target

Brazil's National Consumer Secretariat (Senacon), under the Ministry of Justice, opened an investigation on 6/24 into broadcaster CazeTV for "suspected irregular betting advertising during World Cup broadcasts." CazeTV ran heavy ads for Betnacional, KTO and Bet365 before, during and after matches and displayed odds to viewers. So a probe was opened — and then what? This is the first concrete target of Brazil's World Cup ad crackdown, signaling the government is actually penalizing during the tournament, not just talking. So operators still driving volume via broadcasters and influencers now face real risk. Alongside this, the betting tax (GGR rate) rises from 12% to 13% in July.


Bottom line: -EV for aggressive growth via celebrities, influencers and ads; +EV for licensed operators that keep ads compliant and use owned channels. Action: for Brazil influencer/celebrity deals during the World Cup, first confirm the ad carries the betting regulator's (SPA — the Ministry of Finance betting secretariat) license number and avoids "guaranteed win" / "tied to success" framing — CazeTV is the cautionary example; model the July 13% rate; shift budget from paid celebrity reach to owned channels and licensed media. Signal 05 · Philippines · Regulation −Negative

Philippine legislative proposals: limit e-wallet access to and funding of gambling platforms

The Philippines this week is tightening at the "how players get in" end — mainly two bills still in the legislative stage (note: these are proposals, not yet law): one, Senator Risa Hontiveros's bill to limit access to online gambling via e-wallets and "super apps"; two, the House's House Bill 1351 (the "Kontra E-Sugal" bill), which caps platform access at 21+, bans e-wallets and super apps from promoting gambling content, and bans public-space online-gambling ads. Hontiveros revealed a figure — users can move up to ₱500K (~NT$280K) per day each through GCash and Maya to gambling platforms. A separate, older thread is still counting down: B2B suppliers to local online gambling must complete PAGCOR (Philippine gaming regulator) accreditation by 7/31 — today (6/28) is 33 days out, and unaccredited systems are decommissioned from 8/1.


Bottom line: -EV for small pure-online operators relying on low-friction e-wallet acquisition and unaccredited offshore suppliers, relatively +EV for well-capitalized compliant brands. The reason: the Philippines' two biggest acquisition entry points (instant e-wallet top-ups, no strict age gate) are now both in the legislative line of fire. Action: reconfirm every upstream supplier will complete PAGCOR accreditation before 7/31, and find replacements for any that won't; rebuild acquisition now assuming e-wallet access will be limited and age verification stricter, and harden KYC (know-your-customer) and the age gate; diversify funding rails, don't rely solely on GCash and Maya. Two groups, two product logics.

Market○NeutralUS · Structure

US picture: online casinos legal in just 8 states, online sports betting in ~30 states

CBS Sports · 2026-06

To clear up a common confusion: in the US, "online casino" and "online sports betting" are different things, and states have opened them to very different degrees. Online casino (iCasino — slots, table games) is legal in just 8 states — Connecticut, Delaware, Michigan, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Maine, West Virginia; online sports betting (sportsbook) is legal in ~30 states, far more open. Both are about "have the states legalized it" — and the comparison is: states are willing to open sports betting but far more cautious on online casino. In 2026 the states' legislative focus is responsible gambling and consumer protection, not opening new online casinos.


Bottom line: -EV for those counting on US online-casino expansion, +EV for those deepening in already-open states. Action: don't bet on US online casinos opening widely soon; put US growth into deepening the 8 open states and cross-selling with sports betting; relatively, growth markets like the Philippines and Brazil are more attractive.
