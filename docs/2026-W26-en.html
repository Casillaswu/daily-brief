01 · Lead Regulation ○Neutral North America · Prediction Markets

The prediction-market federal-vs-state fight reaches a red state for the first time: CFTC sues Kentucky on 6/23, the 9th state sued, and Bloomberg reports the same week that the CFTC is itself probing Polymarket

Yogonet · Front Office Sports · Bloomberg · 2026-06-22 ~ 06-28

This week the prediction-market (PMs — platforms that let people bet on whether an event will happen, like Kalshi and Polymarket) litigation took a directional turn. Until now it was Democratic-led states suing the platforms while the CFTC (US Commodity Futures Trading Commission) backed them. On 6/23 the CFTC turned around and sued Kentucky — a Republican-led state whose Attorney General (AG) Russell Coleman is also a Republican. So the fight has reached a red state for the first time, breaking the "only blue states object" pattern. Kentucky is the 9th state the CFTC has sued (after Illinois, Arizona, Connecticut, New York, Rhode Island, Wisconsin, Minnesota and New Mexico); roughly 20 states are now in litigation with prediction-market platforms. Kentucky also added a 14.25% excise tax on prediction-market transactions, which the CFTC's complaint says is designed to make the platforms' business in the state economically impossible. Even more notable: the same week, Bloomberg reported on 6/26 that the CFTC — while defending Polymarket and Kalshi in court — is itself conducting a broad probe into Polymarket.


Bottom line: mixed for the platforms, +EV for licensed operators that pay to stay compliant. Two takeaways. First, the federal government is now suing even a Republican red state, which shows the CFTC wants exclusive jurisdiction, not a partisan side — it doesn't care whether it's suing a blue or red state, it wants the conclusion "PMs are federal, states stay out." Second, even the CFTC, the platforms' supposed protector, is probing Polymarket (Bloomberg 6/26) — which means the line "prediction markets are inherently legal and need no license" doesn't even hold up at its own federal regulator. Action: in the Philippines and Brazil, "the US is fighting prediction-market jurisdiction across 20 states and even the CFTC is probing Polymarket" is ready-made material to argue that "getting licensed and staying compliant is the price of entry, not a disadvantage"; affiliates and B2B partners working with PM platforms should write "US regulation is unsettled, even the federal agency is probing the platform" into contract risk clauses; next, watch the 7/1 Minnesota preliminary-injunction hearing and the end-of-July close of the CFTC comment period.

Signal 02 · Sports Betting ＋Positive

World Cup records move from projection to fact: FanDuel and DraftKings post all-time soccer numbers, DraftKings single-match handle 5x Qatar 2022, plus a "bettors vs prediction traders" behavioral split

Summary: W25 covered the projection for World Cup handle (the total amount bettors wager); this week it became fact. FanDuel (Flutter's brand, US market leader) and DraftKings (DKNG, a top-two US sportsbook) both posted all-time-best soccer numbers — the USA and Brazil opening matches were FanDuel's two most-bet soccer games ever (by active customers), and those same two games set DraftKings single-match handle and active-customer records. On handle alone, DraftKings was 5x the equivalent stage of the 2022 Qatar World Cup. Deutsche Bank puts US tournament handle at $3.3B (~NT$103B) and global wagers above $50B. DraftKings also released comparison data: its sportsbook's most-bet matches were high-profile games and US-team games, but on its prediction product and on Kalshi the most-traded matches were close contests — Kalshi's top list contained no US-team games at all.


Bottom line: +EV for multi-product licensed operators, -EV for soccer-only books, +EV for licensed Brazilian operators (first World Cup since regulation). This week's new thing is real behavioral data — the two user groups want different matches. Traditional bettors chase "my team" and the marquee matchups; prediction traders chase "which result is hardest to call, with the most trading value." That shows PMs aren't simply replacing sportsbooks — they're slicing a different need, more like financial trading. Action: don't only chase marquee games for acquisition; turn "close, hard-to-call" matchups into product hooks too; design the funnel as "register for a match offer, roll into casino," building retention and cross-sell into the flow; keep Philippines and Brazil creative away from "guaranteed win" language and star faces (see Signal 04). Signal 05 · Philippines · Regulation −Negative

Philippine regulation widens from "cleaning the supply chain" to "gating consumer access": Senate and House push two e-wallet and age-limit bills at once, with B2B accreditation 33 days from its 7/31 deadline (T-33)

Summary: W25 covered the B2B supplier accreditation deadline. This week added a layer — regulation widened from "cleaning the supply chain" to "gating how consumers get in." Senator Risa Hontiveros filed a bill to limit access to online gambling via e-wallets and "super apps"; in the House, House Bill 1351 (the "Kontra E-Sugal" bill) goes further — a 21+ age limit, a ban on e-wallets and super apps promoting gambling content, and a ban on public-space online-gambling ads. Hontiveros also revealed a figure: users can move up to ₱500K (~NT$280K) per day each through GCash and Maya to gambling platforms. Add the B2B accreditation 7/31 deadline, now 33 days away (today is 6/28, T-33) — B2B suppliers to local online gambling that fail PAGCOR (Philippine gaming regulator) accreditation by 7/31 will have their systems decommissioned from 8/1.


Bottom line: -EV for small pure-online operators relying on unaccredited offshore suppliers and low-friction e-wallet acquisition; relatively +EV for well-capitalized compliant brands. The new development is that the pressure surface widened — W25 was only the supply chain (B2B accreditation), this week adds the consumer-access end (the two e-wallet and age-limit bills). The Philippines isn't just cleaning suppliers; it's tightening "how players fund accounts and who can play." Action: reconfirm every upstream supplier will complete PAGCOR accreditation before 7/31; an unaccredited supplier means a system shut down 8/1 — find replacements now; rebuild acquisition assuming e-wallet access will be limited and age verification will be stricter, and harden KYC (know-your-customer) and the age gate; recompute unit economics treating both layers (supply-chain accreditation plus access tightening) as fixed cost.
