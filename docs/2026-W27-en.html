01 · LEAD Regulation ○Neutral North America · Prediction Markets

Prediction-market lawsuits: several key nodes this week, all leaning toward "prediction markets are gambling"

Gambling Insider · Courthouse News · 2026-06-29 ~ 07-05

Background: who regulates prediction markets (PMs — platforms letting people bet on whether an event will happen, like Kalshi and Polymarket) has been argued for half a year. The federal financial regulator, the CFTC (Commodity Futures Trading Commission), says "these are financial products, federal jurisdiction"; many states say "this is gambling, our jurisdiction, ban it."

Several key nodes this week:
1. Michigan: a judge issued a temporary restraining order (TRO) requiring Kalshi to stop offering sports-event contracts in the state, with continuing fines if it doesn't. Michigan is the 3rd state with an injunction against Kalshi, after Nevada and Massachusetts.
2. Nevada: the Supreme Court denied Kalshi's emergency stay and scheduled a July 16 contempt hearing (over why it didn't geofence out state users).
3. Platforms fight back: Polymarket sued New Mexico in federal court on July 1; Kalshi separately sued Ohio's casino regulator to void a $5M penalty.
4. New Jersey: the case reached the U.S. Supreme Court — Justice Alito gave NJ until Aug 4 to file its petition. This could be SCOTUS's first word on whether federal financial law preempts state gambling law.

In the same week's Minnesota federal hearing (July 3), Judge Menendez asked whether sports-event contracts are meaningfully different from traditional sports betting, and called it a hard sell to convince her that betting on a player's rebound total has economic meaning; she didn't rule that day and took it under advisement.


Bottom line: -EV for prediction-market platforms, +EV for licensed operators who pay to play by the rules. With this many courts — including a Republican-run state and state regulators — pushing back on PMs, the direction is clear: the view that "prediction markets are gambling" is strengthening, and the claim that "they're inherently legal financial products, no license needed" is weakening. For emerging-market operators in the Philippines and Brazil, the direct takeaway: don't believe "PMs can run without a license" — licensing and compliance are the price of entry in this business. Action: agents and B2Bs partnering with PM platforms should write "US regulation unsettled, state-level injunctions and fines ongoing" into contract risk clauses and spell out who bears the loss when a state bans; watch three dates — July 16 Nevada contempt hearing, Aug 1 Minnesota ban effective, Aug 4 NJ Supreme Court deadline.

Signal 03 · Brazil · Regulation −Negative

Brazil's Ministry of Finance opens proceedings July 1, orders CazéTV ads pulled immediately; penalties up to license loss or R$2B

Brazil's betting regulatory secretariat (SPA, Secretaria de Prêmios e Apostas, under the Ministry of Finance) opened proceedings July 1 against multiple betting companies over their ads on World Cup broadcaster CazéTV. The grounds are specific: the ads "encouraged an urge to bet," and the mandatory gambling-risk warnings were shown in illegible size. SPA also issued a precautionary order to suspend the offending ads immediately — unlike the ad self-regulator CONAR (advisory only), the Ministry's order is legally binding. The Ministry of Justice also stepped in over Consumer Protection Code violations. Penalties: for serious or repeat offenses, up to license revocation or a R$2B fine. Separately, the betting tax (GGR rate) rose from 12% to 13% in July (15% by 2028); yet H1 tax revenue hit a record — betting alone contributed BRL5.89B in the first five months, up nearly 86% YoY.


Bottom line: -EV for celebrity/influencer/odds-in-broadcast volume plays, +EV for licensed operators who keep ads compliant via owned channels. Easy to misread: record tax revenue doesn't mean lighter regulation — it gives the government confidence that "this industry has money, so regulate and tax more." Scrutiny went from investigating one broadcaster to the Ministry itself opening proceedings and ordering ads pulled — the regulator is serious this time. Action: for Brazil influencer/celebrity deals during the World Cup, confirm the ad carries the SPA license number and doesn't disguise odds as commentary — CazéTV is the caught sample; bake the 13% rate and the path to 15% by 2028 into the model.iGaming Brazil · Read original →

Signal 05 · Asia · Regulation −Negative

Philippines B2B deadline 26 days out, e-wallet bills advancing; Vietnam busts a Taiwanese-led gambling and laundering ring

Philippines: B2B suppliers to local online betting must complete PAGCOR (the Philippine gaming regulator) accreditation by July 31; today (July 5) is 26 days out, and unaccredited systems are force-shut Aug 1. Two bills (limiting e-wallet access to betting, setting a 21+ age gate — Senator Hontiveros's bill and House Bill 1351 "Kontra E-Sugal") keep drawing debate in Congress, both tightening the "how players get in" end.
Vietnam (new watch market): police busted a transnational online gambling-and-laundering ring led by 5 Taiwanese nationals, using 266 bank accounts to launder over VNĐ1 trillion (about US$38.4M) in two months, with 66 officers arresting 36 people across Ho Chi Minh City, Hanoi, Ha Tinh and other areas. Vietnam's only legal options are local pilots — Corona Resort (Phú Quốc), Grand Ho Tram, Vân Đồn — with the offshore grey market (W88, WE88, 188bet) still dominant but enforcement tightening.


Bottom line: -EV for small pure-online operators relying on low-friction e-wallet acquisition or unaccredited offshore suppliers, -EV for offshore grey-market brands in Vietnam, relatively +EV for well-capitalized brands that can absorb compliance costs. Philippines and Vietnam are two cases of the same direction — regulation shifting from "licensing" to "enforcement, closing entry points." Action: in the Philippines, re-confirm each upstream supplier's July 31 PAGCOR accreditation and line up replacements now; rebuild acquisition assuming e-wallet access gets restricted and age verification tightens; for Vietnam, treat "offshore networks getting hit by enforcement" as an explicit operating premise.Vietnam News · Read original →

Market-integrity alert: a Kalshi market on "will a song hit Spotify's #1" drew bots to fake 500K streams

Gambling Insider · 2026-07

Spotify removed over 500,000 fake streams of the song "Earrings" — because a Kalshi market on "will this song hit Spotify's US #1" drew bots to inflate streams and manipulate the outcome, actually pushing the song to #1. It started with a Kalshi trader complaining that bot activity had distorted the market.


Bottom line: -EV for PM platforms. A hard example of "can PMs prevent manipulation" — when a market's payout ties to a spammable real-world number (streams, votes), people will spam that number to win. That's a structural difference from traditional sports betting: match results are hard to fix, but "a chart position" is easy. Action: strong material for arguing "PMs carry integrity risks traditional betting doesn't" — useful when explaining to regulators or partners why licensed sports betting is more controllable.
