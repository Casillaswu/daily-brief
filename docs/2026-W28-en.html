01 · LEAD Regulation ○Neutral North America · Prediction Markets

New York federal court denies Kalshi: federal commodities law does not preempt state gambling law — lawmakers pivot to the marketing and payments ecosystem

Crowdfund Insider · SBC Americas · Crypto Briefing · 07/07 ~ 07/11

On 7 July, Judge Torres of the Southern District of New York denied Kalshi's request for a preliminary injunction. Kalshi is a CFTC-licensed prediction market — a platform where people bet on whether a given event will happen. Her reasoning was blunt: the Commodity Exchange Act does not displace state gambling law, and federal authority applies only where Congress has explicitly taken over. In plain terms, a federal licence does not stop New York from applying its gambling law to Kalshi's sports contracts.

The ruling lands inside a bigger picture. From 8 to 11 July the annual meeting of US state legislators (NCLGS) took place in San Diego, and prediction markets were the very first session. Former White House chief of staff Mick Mulvaney made three points that matter: Congress will not deliver clarifying legislation, the odds of the CFTC (Commodity Futures Trading Commission) reversing its position are low, and this will most likely end up at the US Supreme Court. A second takeaway matters even more — since suing platforms directly has limited effect, attendees urged legislators to go after the supporting ecosystem: marketing affiliates, media partners and payment processors. Google moved the same week: its 1 July update to Chrome Web Store developer policy explicitly bans extensions that let users place real-money prediction-market bets inside the browser, with enforcement starting 1 August.


Bottom line: -EV for prediction-market platforms, +EV for licensed operators who pay for a licence, but a new -EV risk for mid-stream players doing affiliate marketing, media partnerships and payment integration. For the past few weeks the story was "states are suing the platforms." This week the middlemen who work with the platforms — marketing, media, payments — started getting named too: the risk moves from "my client might get fined" to "I might get fined." Google's move is the first time a non-government platform has swept prediction markets off its own distribution channel — and platform policy usually bites faster than a court ruling, because there is no trial to wait for. Action: if you have any partnership with a prediction-market platform (affiliate traffic, content placement, payment rails), write into the contract now who bears the loss when a state bans it or a platform delists it. Watch three dates: 7/27 CFTC public-interest determination comment deadline, 7/31 CFTC data reporting comment deadline, 8/1 Minnesota ban takes effect on the same day Google enforcement begins.

SIGNAL 02 · NORTH AMERICA · TAX

North Carolina lifts online sportsbook tax from 18% to 23% — and taxes prediction markets at just 6%

Governor Josh Stein signed the new state budget on 7/9. Two lines sit side by side: online sports betting revenue tax rises from 18% to 23%; prediction markets are taxed at 6%, with no licensing or regulatory requirement attached. Licensed operators face vetting, bonds and responsible-gambling duties. Prediction markets just pay 6%.


Bottom line: -EV for licensed sportsbook operators, +EV for prediction markets. Same state, same players — and the licensed side pays nearly 4x the tax. This is the first time regulatory arbitrage has been written into a state budget in quantified form. Action: file this "tax first, regulate later" sequence into your emerging-market model. The Philippines and Brazil both walked the same path. When assessing market entry, do not just ask "is it legal" — ask whether the tax rate and the regulatory burden are proportionate. SIGNAL 04 · BRAZIL · REGULATION

Brazil's new ad rules land 7/17: commentators and celebrities can no longer urge bets, every ad must carry a warning

Brazil's new advertising rules take effect 7/17, two days before the World Cup final (7/19). Every ad by a licensed operator must carry one of three finance-ministry warnings (betting causes losses, betting can cause dependency, betting is not an investment); betting may not be portrayed as an easy way to make money; commentators, celebrities and public figures may not encourage betting in content. The same week the finance ministry notified 37 fintechs to stop servicing roughly 160 illegal betting sites by the end of August.


Bottom line: -EV for brands built on celebrity endorsement and aggressive voice-over, +EV for brands that already built compliant warnings into their creative templates. The timing is deliberate — the government wants the single biggest traffic wave of the year to run under the new rules. Action: rebuild every Brazilian asset scheduled after 7/17 with warnings baked in; put an explicit list of forbidden phrases into celebrity contracts rather than letting talent improvise on camera. World Cup handle: US full-tournament estimate $2.82B to $4.3B — 9x Qatar

Inside World Football · 07/08

US licensed sportsbooks are projected to handle between US$2.82 billion and US$4.3 billion (about NT$89–135 billion) across the tournament — nine times the roughly US$490 million wagered on Qatar 2022. New Jersey, Pennsylvania and Illinois, the three largest legal markets outside Nevada, each posted single-day handle during group-stage matches exceeding their previous records from the NFL playoffs and March Madness. Two drivers: legal mobile betting now live in 39 states, up from around 30 last cycle; and the format expanding to 48 teams and 104 matches. Reports on 7/9 noted that the US team's elimination pushed handle higher still.


Bottom line: +EV for full-service operators. The World Cup proved one thing — football can reach real scale in the US. The constraint was never "Americans don't watch football," it was "there weren't enough legal channels." Semifinals on 7/14 and 7/15 and the final on 7/19 (MetLife Stadium, New Jersey) are the last two waves.

Sports○Neutral📌 Editor's Pick

Sports theme: the World Cup put a number on "not enough legal channels"

📍 Editorial synthesis from §3, §8 this issue — representative source below

US handle is nine times the last cycle, and the reason is not that nine times as many football fans appeared. It is that legal states went from 30 to 39 and the schedule went from 64 matches to 104. Apply that logic to the Philippines and Brazil: demand for football and basketball betting was always there; what was suppressed was the channel. Whichever brand first builds a legal, low-friction, three-taps-on-mobile path captures that suppressed demand first.


Bottom line: +EV for brands investing properly in distribution. But look at the flip side — the reason prediction markets could pull US$33 billion of volume from the same World Cup is that their friction is lower than the licensed side's, with no KYC and no deposit caps. Low friction cuts both ways: the more compliance you carry, the more friction you carry, and the easier it becomes for a grey competitor to take your marginal users. That is the hardest problem the licensed side faces over the next two years.
