01 · Lead Product ○Neutral N. America · Prediction markets

The World Cup final pushes prediction markets to a record high: Kalshi's single contract tops $1.27B

Fortune · CoinDesk · Benzinga · 07/14 ~ 07/17

Kalshi is a US federally licensed platform (licensed by the CFTC, the Commodity Futures Trading Commission) running "prediction markets" (PMs) — letting people bet on whether an event will happen, including football results. The World Cup final kicks off 19 July at MetLife Stadium in New Jersey, Argentina vs Spain.

That final became the largest single market prediction markets have ever seen: Kalshi's Argentina-vs-Spain final contract — a bet on who wins this one match — topped $1.27B in volume by 7/17. World Cup contracts across the tournament traded over $25B combined, against roughly $2B for the NBA Finals and $1B for the Super Bowl. Polymarket's $4.2B (the crypto-settled offshore platform, now with a US entity) sits on a different market: the outright-winner market — open since before kickoff, every team on the board — not the final itself.

Licensed operators are not standing still. DraftKings (DKNG, a top-two US sportsbook) used its acquired Railbird (a CFTC-licensed platform) to build its own prediction exchange DKeX, wired straight into the DraftKings app. Its prediction business ran an annualized $3.4B in consumer volume and $11.3B in trading volume during the World Cup, with first-time customers up 3x and volume up 87%, combos about 40% of the mix.


Bottom line: -EV for pure licensed sportsbooks, +EV for operators that also built prediction products. The World Cup laid bare the friction gap — bettors on prediction markets skip KYC and deposit caps, so money flows there. Licensed operators can't win the cost war on the sports-betting line; DraftKings' choice shows the direction — if you can't beat them, build your own. But regulators hit back hard the same week (see Signal 02), so this isn't a one-sided PM win — peak demand and peak enforcement collided on the same weekend. Action: pure online sportsbooks should move "build or partner on a PM-style product" from "watch" to "decide this year," while watching the regulatory counterforce closely.

SIGNAL 02 · International · Regulation

France cuts the network: orders ISPs to block Polymarket nationwide

France's national gambling authority (ANJ) ordered ISPs to block Polymarket nationwide on 7/16 — an escalation after a payment ban failed to stop traffic (578,751 visits last month). Promoting unlicensed gambling is a criminal offence in France, fined up to €100,000 (~NT$3.5M). Polymarket is now restricted in 36 jurisdictions, including Spain, Singapore and Brazil.


Bottom line: -EV for pure offshore prediction platforms, +EV for licensed operators. The new thing is the level of the tool — the US route is state lawsuits that wait for rulings; France simply ordered ISPs to cut the line overnight. Action: if Philippines or Vietnam copy the ISP-block playbook, the lifeline offshore platforms rely on for traffic gets cut. Audit any traffic deals with unlicensed platforms now. Read original →

SIGNAL 03 · LatAm · Brazil

Brazil tightens on three fronts: Rio becomes the first state capital to ban public-space betting ads

Rio de Janeiro published Decree Rio 58.274/2026 on 7/13, banning fixed-odds betting ads in outdoor media and any space needing municipal approval (billboards, street furniture, brand names, logos, mascots), with a non-extendable 10-day removal window. Meanwhile federal ad-warning rules went live 7/17, and the Supreme Court (STF) decided on 7/15 to fast-track key betting cases in H2.


Bottom line: -EV for brands leaning on outdoor placements and celebrity call-outs, +EV for those with compliance baked into templates. Enforcement went from one federal line to three simultaneous layers (federal rules, municipal ads, courts). Action: Rio outdoor placements must move within 10 days; build national creative to the strictest state. Read original →

No more waiting on courts: France blocks the network, Google pulls the listing

📍 Editorial synthesis from §1, §6, §7 — representative source below

Two different places, two different tools: France used an administrative order to make ISPs block Polymarket nationwide; Google used its own store policy to delist betting extensions. US states are still suing (slowly — Kalshi keeps operating), but France has set the precedent: if you want speed, go for the entry points.


Bottom line: +EV for pure licensed operators, -EV for offshore and unlicensed platforms. Anyone building on Philippine or Vietnamese traffic should add "regulator copies the French block" to the risk list — a network block has no grace period, and offshore-fed traffic deals go to zero overnight.

Product○Neutral📌 Editor's Pick

Two faces of prediction markets: built like a financial product, used like a betting shop

📍 Editorial synthesis from §3, §7 — W28 view held

Two layers are moving separately. In product design, these platforms keep adding features only financial exchanges have (Polymarket's margin filing, Kalshi's leverage). In actual usage, the bulk of volume is World Cup sports betting — users treat it as a betting shop. Built like finance, used like gambling — which is exactly why it's hard to regulate from either side — going financial hits leverage and suitability rules, going gambling hits state betting law, and now France adds a third pressure by cutting the network outright. No new thesis needed this week, view held.


Bottom line: +EV for licensed operators. Their moat is "regulatory certainty" — knowing who regulates them, what they owe, what they can do. Prediction markets now face uncertainty on both US and European fronts, and that uncertainty ultimately becomes their cost.
